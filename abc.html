Gardening & Composting Tips

Indoor Plants

Examples: Snake Plant, Money Plant, Peace Lily, Spider Plant, Pothos

Light Requirements: Most thrive in bright, indirect light. Avoid direct sunlight.

Watering: Water when top inch of soil is dry. Overwatering is a common killer.

Placement: Living room, bedroom, office desk, bathrooms with natural light.

Outdoor Plants

Examples: Hibiscus, Rose, Jasmine, Marigold, Sunflower

Light Requirements: Most need 6+ hours of direct sunlight daily.

Watering: Water deeply but less frequently to encourage deep roots.

Placement: Balcony, terrace edges, garden pots, flower beds.

Home Farming

Examples: Tomatoes, Spinach, Mint, Lemons, Bell Peppers, Herbs

Soil: Use organic soil mix rich in compost for best results.

Watering: Keep soil consistently moist but not waterlogged.

Placement: Kitchen garden, rooftops, backyard, sunny windowsills.

Composting Guide

What to Compost:
  • Fruit and vegetable scraps
  • Coffee grounds and filters
  • Eggshells (crushed)
  • Grass clippings and leaves
  • Shredded newspaper and cardboard
  • Tea bags (remove staples)
What to Avoid:
  • Dairy products
  • Meat, fish, and bones
  • Fats, grease, and oils
  • Diseased plants
  • Pet wastes
  • Coal or charcoal ash

Complete Indoor Plant Care Guide
Best Indoor Plants for Beginners:
  • Snake Plant: Thrives in low light, requires minimal watering
  • Pothos: Fast-growing, tolerates various light conditions
  • ZZ Plant: Extremely drought-tolerant, perfect for forgetful waterers
  • Spider Plant: Produces baby plants, purifies air effectively
  • Peace Lily: Blooms indoors, indicates when it needs water
Light Requirements:

Most indoor plants prefer bright, indirect light. East-facing windows are ideal. Rotate plants regularly for even growth.

Watering Schedule:

Check soil moisture by inserting finger 1-2 inches deep. Water only when dry. Overwatering is the most common cause of indoor plant death.

Fertilizing:

Use balanced liquid fertilizer diluted to half strength during growing season (spring and summer). Reduce or stop in winter.

Complete Outdoor Plant Care Guide
Best Outdoor Plants for Different Conditions:
  • Full Sun: Lavender, Rosemary, Sunflowers, Marigolds
  • Partial Shade: Fuchsia, Impatiens, Begonias, Hostas
  • Shade: Ferns, Heuchera, Bleeding Heart, Astilbe
Seasonal Care:

Spring: Prune winter damage, apply compost, plant new additions

Summer: Regular watering, deadheading flowers, pest monitoring

Fall: Plant spring bulbs, reduce watering, prepare for winter

Winter: Protect sensitive plants, plan next season's garden

Soil Preparation:

Mix compost into planting holes. For containers, use high-quality potting mix with good drainage.

Pest Management:

Encourage beneficial insects like ladybugs. Use neem oil or insecticidal soap for outbreaks. Practice crop rotation for vegetables.

Complete Home Farming Guide
Easy Vegetables for Beginners:
  • Leafy Greens: Lettuce, Spinach, Kale - fast growing, continuous harvest
  • Herbs: Basil, Mint, Cilantro - grow well in containers
  • Root Vegetables: Radishes, Carrots - direct sow, minimal maintenance
  • Tomatoes: Cherry varieties most successful for beginners
Container Gardening Tips:

Choose containers with drainage holes. Use high-quality potting mix. Size matters - larger containers hold moisture better and allow for more root growth.

Succession Planting:

Plant new seeds every 2-3 weeks for continuous harvest. This works especially well for leafy greens, radishes, and beans.

Companion Planting:

Plant basil near tomatoes to repel pests. Marigolds throughout the garden deter nematodes. Avoid planting beans near onions.

Harvesting Tips:

Harvest leafy greens in the morning when they're crispest. Pick tomatoes when fully colored but still firm. Regular harvesting encourages more production.

Complete Composting Guide
The Composting Process:

Composting is nature's way of recycling organic materials into nutrient-rich soil amendment. The process relies on microorganisms that break down materials.

Setting Up Your Compost Bin:
  1. Choose a location with partial shade and good drainage
  2. Start with a layer of coarse materials like small twigs for aeration
  3. Add alternating layers of green (nitrogen-rich) and brown (carbon-rich) materials
  4. Maintain moisture like a wrung-out sponge
  5. Turn the pile every 1-2 weeks to introduce oxygen
Green Materials (Nitrogen):
  • Fruit and vegetable scraps
  • Coffee grounds and tea bags
  • Fresh grass clippings
  • Plant trimmings
Brown Materials (Carbon):
  • Dry leaves
  • Straw or hay
  • Shredded paper and cardboard
  • Sawdust (from untreated wood)
  • Egg cartons
Troubleshooting Common Issues:

Smelly compost: Too wet or not enough air - add brown materials and turn

Not heating up: Needs more green materials or water - add nitrogen sources

Attracting pests: Avoid adding meat, dairy, or oily foods

When is Compost Ready?

Compost is ready when it's dark, crumbly, and has an earthy smell. This typically takes 2-6 months depending on conditions and maintenance.
